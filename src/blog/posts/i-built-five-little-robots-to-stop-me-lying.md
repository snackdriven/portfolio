<p class="lead">Working with Claude all day, the failure isn&rsquo;t that it&rsquo;s dumb. It&rsquo;s that it&rsquo;s confident, and so am I, and confidence at the finish line walks you straight past the moment you were supposed to check. I declared one refactor done three separate times. It was wrong three separate times. So I started building gates.</p>

There are five, and each one exists because of a specific time I got burned. One won&rsquo;t let me claim a ticket&rsquo;s status without a receipt from the actual source, because I once put three false facts straight into a stakeholder doc. One blocks &ldquo;ready for QA&rdquo; unless it came from the real readiness script, because I once said five tickets were ready when the script said zero. One blocks a Jira comment that skipped the voice pass. One stops two terminals running the same job at once. One won&rsquo;t let me type &ldquo;done&rdquo; without proof something ran.

The gates are deliberately stupid. They don&rsquo;t read my work and judge it. They check for a little receipt token, that&rsquo;s it. The point is forcing a conscious decision at the exact spot I&rsquo;d otherwise sail past. They also fail open, so a broken gate lets the work through instead of blocking everything. A safety rail that turns into a roadblock gets ripped out by lunch.

My favorite watches the others. When I save the same lesson to memory for the fourth time, it tells me to stop writing notes and build a gate instead.

I automated my own conscience because the manual one kept calling in sick.
