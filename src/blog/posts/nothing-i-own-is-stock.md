<p class="lead">I have never owned a piece of technology I left the way it came. I went looking for an exception while writing this. There isn&rsquo;t one.</p>

It starts early and it never stops. The first phone I was allowed to have got a ringtone I made myself, badly, out of a song I didn&rsquo;t own. The iPod got jailbroken the same week the warranty would have covered whatever I did to it. Every Android I&rsquo;ve held has run something other than what it shipped with &mdash; I have flashed more firmware than some people have owned phones. There is a specific moment a device becomes actually mine, and it is not the moment I pay for it. It&rsquo;s the first time I change something the manufacturer would rather I didn&rsquo;t.

For years I told myself this was about features. The stock launcher was slow, the default keyboard was cramped, the watchface was ugly &mdash; all true, all beside the point. The tell is that I keep doing it to things that are already fine. The [Pebble](/true-love-never-dies.html) was a perfect little watch and I buried it under faces I wrote anyway. The [AL80](/i-reverse-engineered-the-screen-on-an-impulse-keyboard.html) came with a working clock and I still spent a night in its HID traffic to turn a 17 into a 5. Nobody asked me to. The device was fine. I was the problem.

The honest version is that I don&rsquo;t quite trust anything I can&rsquo;t take apart. A device I can&rsquo;t change is a device that can change on me: a feature pulled in an update, a server switched off, a subscription bolted onto something I already bought and paid for. I&rsquo;ve watched it happen enough times to treat stock as a temporary condition. Fitbit switched off my watch. An update deleted my [terminal pet](/unknown-skill-buddy.html). A vendor decided a 12-hour clock was too much to ask. The mod isn&rsquo;t vanity. It&rsquo;s insurance.

So I keep a tally, because of course I do. Sixteen devices in active rotation, thirteen of them running something they weren&rsquo;t sold with, and a drawer &mdash; everyone has the drawer &mdash; holding the twenty-two that got modded, outgrown, and retired with dignity. The phone runs a launcher its maker never shipped. The watch shows faces I wrote. The keyboard spins a skull where the vendor wanted a logo. The terminal has a bat named Flicker. Even the laptop&rsquo;s entire personality is a pile of scripts, a few of which have genuinely tried to brick it. The through-line is embarrassing in its consistency: I cannot leave a working thing alone.

You&rsquo;re reading the largest mod I&rsquo;ve pulled yet. This site is hand-built, no template, and there&rsquo;s a resume hiding in the full stop of the domain at the bottom of the page because a plain link felt too obvious. I couldn&rsquo;t leave the blog stock either. Somewhere there is a version of me who buys a thing, uses it exactly as intended, and gets on with her day. I&rsquo;ve never met her. I assume she came that way from the factory.
