<p class="lead">I have never owned a piece of technology I left the way it came. I went looking for an exception while writing this. There isn&rsquo;t one.</p>

It started before I had the skill for it. The earliest evidence is the small CRT television that lived in my childhood bedroom, onto which I wrote my name in yellow colored pencil and got fussed at accordingly. I didn&rsquo;t have a mod in me yet. I had a name, a surface, and the unshakeable instinct that the two belonged together.

The skill came later, and it came hardest with the Droid. A custom bootloader first, then a firmware that wasn&rsquo;t Motorola&rsquo;s, then the specific thrill of a boot animation that played because I put it there. Somewhere in that phone the whole thing leveled up: from writing on a surface to replacing the system underneath it. Every device since has been a variation on the same two moves &mdash; claim the surface, swap what shipped. I have flashed more firmware than some people have owned phones. And there is a precise moment a device becomes actually mine, one that has never once been the moment I paid for it. It&rsquo;s the first time I change something the manufacturer would rather I hadn&rsquo;t.

For years I told myself this was about features. The stock launcher was slow, the default keyboard was cramped, the watchface was ugly &mdash; all true, all beside the point. The tell is that I keep doing it to things that are already fine. The [Pebble](/true-love-never-dies.html) was a perfect little watch and I buried it under faces I wrote anyway. The [AL80](/i-reverse-engineered-the-screen-on-an-impulse-keyboard.html) came with a working clock and I still spent a night in its HID traffic to turn a 17 into a 5. Nobody asked me to. The device was fine. I was the problem.

The honest version is that I don&rsquo;t quite trust anything I can&rsquo;t take apart. A device I can&rsquo;t change is a device that can change on me: a feature pulled in an update, a server switched off, a subscription bolted onto something I already bought and paid for. I&rsquo;ve watched it happen enough times to treat stock as a temporary condition. Fitbit switched off my watch. An update deleted my [terminal pet](/unknown-skill-buddy.html). A vendor decided a 12-hour clock was too much to ask. The mod isn&rsquo;t vanity. It&rsquo;s insurance.

So I keep a tally, because of course I do. Sixteen devices in active rotation, thirteen of them running something they weren&rsquo;t sold with, and a backstock of twenty-two more I cannot bring myself to part with: jailbroken Kindles running firmware Amazon never blessed, a Vita on homebrew doing what Sony spent real money to prevent, a graveyard of phones each frozen on the exact custom ROM I loved it on. Nothing gets thrown out. It gets shelved, modded and mothballed, like a museum with a single unhinged curator. The phone in my pocket runs a launcher its maker never shipped. The watch shows faces I wrote. The keyboard spins a skull where the vendor wanted a logo. The terminal runs Oh My Posh with a prompt I&rsquo;ve retuned more times than I&rsquo;ll admit &mdash; glyphs, colors, segments, the works &mdash; and that&rsquo;s all before I wired a bat named Flicker into the Claude Code statusline to change his face when I commit, push, or land a big edit. Even the laptop&rsquo;s entire personality is a pile of scripts, a few of which have genuinely tried to brick it. The through-line is embarrassing in its consistency: I cannot leave a working thing alone.

You&rsquo;re reading the largest mod I&rsquo;ve pulled yet. This site is hand-built, no template, and there&rsquo;s a resume hiding in the full stop of the domain at the bottom of the page because a plain link felt too obvious. I couldn&rsquo;t leave the blog stock either. Somewhere there is a version of me who buys a thing, uses it exactly as intended, and gets on with her day. I&rsquo;ve never met her. I assume she came that way from the factory.
