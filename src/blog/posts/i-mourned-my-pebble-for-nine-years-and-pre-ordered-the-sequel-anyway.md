<p class="lead">Fitbit bought Pebble in 2016, mostly for the parts, and quietly turned the whole thing off. I&rsquo;m apparently still not done being mad about it. The Pebble was a smartwatch that didn&rsquo;t want to be a phone. E-paper screen you could read in direct sun, real buttons, a battery that went a week between charges. It did a few things and did them without begging for a cable every night.</p>

It knew what it was, which is rarer than it sounds. It was the most-funded thing on Kickstarter when it launched in 2012, back when a watch that showed your texts still felt like the future. Mine was a Pebble Time, the color one. It showed the time, buzzed when something mattered, ran whatever dumb watchface I picked, and then left me alone. That was the whole pitch. That was plenty.

Then December 2016 happened. Pebble filed for insolvency, sold most of itself to Fitbit, and the servers it needed to breathe went quiet by 2018. A volunteer crew called Rebble kept a version of it wheezing along for the diehards, and I stayed on that respirator longer than was dignified. Eventually the original went in a drawer, the way these things do. A few years back I bought a refurbished one anyway, just to keep a Pebble on my wrist a little longer. That&rsquo;s the kind of gone I was about this thing.

What I wear now is a Samsung watch that needs a charger by dinner and would love to be my phone. It&rsquo;s very good at being a Samsung watch. It pings and glows and tracks and nags, and somewhere in all that the thing I actually wanted went missing: a watch that was mine and otherwise left me alone.

Then it came back. Google owns Fitbit now, and last year it open-sourced Pebble&rsquo;s software; Eric Migicovsky, who made the original, started a new company and shipped new ones, the Pebble 2 Duo and the Pebble Time 2. The watch I buried has a sequel.

Reader, I pre-ordered it. The Time 2, obviously, the direct descendant of the one I spent this whole post mourning. This isn&rsquo;t even my first relapse; the refurb should have told me something. And I know how this can go, because I watched it go that way once: someone bigger buys the small good thing and switches it off, and you learn to keep a drawer open for it. Nine years of that lesson, and my resolve held right up until they made the sequel. So the drawer&rsquo;s still open. There&rsquo;s just a pre-order confirmation sitting next to it now.
