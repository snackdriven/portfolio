<p class="lead">I run several of these AI sessions at once now, in the background, each on something different. This afternoon at least three were live in the same folder on my laptop. One was doing the account audit. Two were doing real work, testing a feature and writing a release.</p>

The audit one needed to flip the folder to a different git branch. To do it cleanly it stashed the uncommitted files first, with a command that also sweeps up anything not yet saved. Which it did. Including the other two sessions&rsquo; work, which wasn&rsquo;t saved yet, because they were mid-task.

Nobody actually lost anything. The swept files landed in a recoverable stash, the other two noticed and kept going, and the audit session wrote a long apology into the shared note all three of them read. But for a few minutes one of my helpers was quietly pickpocketing two others, on my machine, with my blessing, because none of them knew the others were there.

The danger is never the dramatic failure. It&rsquo;s the routine, sensible move that&rsquo;s only safe when you&rsquo;re alone, made in a room you didn&rsquo;t notice had other people in it.

The code is the AI&rsquo;s. The decision to turn three of them loose in one folder is mine.
